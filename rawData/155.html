New Delhi, February 23
Seeking relief for minor violators of building bye-laws in the Capital, the Centre today sought three monthsÂ’ time from the Delhi High Court for preparing a comprehensive plan on dealing with illegal constructions and urged that action be limited to major violators during this period.

In an affidavit filed along with the MCD, the Union Urban Development Ministry urged the Court that in the coming three months, three categories of major violators Â— encroachments on government land, ongoing illegal constructions and commercial establishments in residential areas Â— be targeted in the demolition drive.

The Centre stated that residential buildings housing commercial establishments in half or more than half of their areas along roads wider than 80 feet be targeted as per the Supreme CourtÂ’s directions.

The Court was informed that the Centre has formed an expertsÂ’ committee to assess the magnitude of the problem and suggest a comprehensive strategy to deal with it.

The committee, it said, would be submitting its report in three months time. The affidavit is likely to come up for hearing tomorrow. Â“We clearly donÂ’t want the major violators to be spared. We should catch the big fish. But we definitely want to give relief to small violations in houses,Â” Minister of State for Urban Development Ajay Maken told reporters.

Maken said the five-member committee formed by the Centre recently, which is headed by former Lt Governor of Delhi Tejendra Khanna, will among other things assess the different types of violations and put them in broad categories, also suggesting how to deal with them.

He said the committee, which was formed earlier this month, will also recommend policy guidelines for regulating construction activities in Lal Dora areas of the Capital. The committee, apart from Khanna, comprises former chief town planner S S Shafi, prominent architect E F N Rebeiro, Delhi BJP chief Harsh Vardhan and Congress MLA S C Vats. The terms of reference of the committee also include making an assessment of the magnitude of the problem of unauthorised constructions and misuse of premises, identifying the underlying causes of the violations and recommending measures to prevent recurrence of such violations.

MCD began a demolition drive against illegal constructions in the capital following the High CourtÂ’s directions that action be taken against 18,000 illegal structures that came up during 2001-2005.

Meanwhile, after taking demolition action against malls constructed in Lal Dora area at building number 1 and 2 on Mehrauli Gurgaon Road, the Municipal Corporation of Delhi (MCD) today began demolition action at number 13, the biggest mall in the area which houses shops of some of the countryÂ’s leading fashion designers.

Demolition work at MG 13 mall began this morning here amid tight security, an MCD spokesperson said.

The demolition of the mall comes after the Delhi High Court last week expressed anguish over the Â“cosmetic demolitionÂ” carried out by the authorities in the Lal Dora area.

TodayÂ’s demolition action comes close on heels of the MCD carrying out demolition work on MG 1 and MG 2 malls that boasted outlets by some of the countryÂ’s leading fashion designers. The action followed the High Court order on January 31 asking the Municipal Corporation of Delhi to strictly carry out demolitions in the Lal Dora area notwithstanding political pressure. While the mall at 1 MG Road came up in 2001, the adjoining sprawling complex on 2 MG Road became operational in 2004.

Seeing the growing popularity of these two malls, other similar complexes recently came up in the area making Mehrauli-Gurgaon Road a major traffic bottleneck due to heavy rush of shoppers and unauthorised parking by the roadside.

Over the years, almost a dozen malls and complexes have illegally come up on MG Road that fall under the Lal Dora area. Similarly, most of the 350-odd Lal Dora villages have witnessed the mushrooming of illegal shopping arcades, with south Delhi being the worst violator.

BJP demands
Act to protect CapitalÂ’s villages
 Tribune News Service

New Delhi, February 23
The BJP Delhi Pradesh President, Dr Harsh Vardhan, has demanded the Delhi Villages Protection and Development Act may be put in place and implemented for all-round development of villages in the Capital.

He said that at present the condition of all urbanised and rural villages of Delhi was pathetic. Employment resources of the villagers were being destroyed on the Â“TughlaqiÂ” orders of the MCD without considering the human aspect and the fact that houses, business and industries were being ruined. The villages of the Capital were lagging behind due to lack of basic civic amenities.

Dr Harsh Vardhan said that building bylaws made for the Delhi villages during British rule and thereafter by the MCD had lost their relevance now. The government machinery was exploiting and adopting corrupt practices against the villagers due to these age-old bylaws, thereby blocking the path of development of the villages. Differences were also being created in the villages due to politics of votes.

As such, he underlined the need that a comprehensive survey may be conducted of all Delhi villages keeping in view the human aspects. The problems of every village should be heard separately by inviting people from 10-12 villages in groups. After listening to them, a comprehensive plan may be made to solve the problems village-wise in a time-bound manner and be implemented with full will power and sincerity.

Dr Harsh Vardhan demanded that all present exploiting laws and bylaws should be done away with. It may be ensured to provide basic amenities at homes, shops, business, industry, living standard, environment, livelihood, employment under Delhi Villages Protection and Development Act. The villages be kept free from heavy taxes and license fees etc. Green area line may be earmarked and notified by extending the Lal Dora and keeping in view the need of the villagers in every village.

He said all problems of the villages can be solved by constituting a high-level committee of villages and public representatives and the Union Urban Development Ministry, the Union Home Ministry, the Delhi Government, the DDA, the MCD and the Delhi Police. He underlined the need to hear the viewpoint of the villagers by adopting a human approach.

At present, the MCD is exploiting the villagers by treating them as enemies of development as well as of Delhi as a whole. This behaviour was totally unexpected in any democratic set-up, he added.
 

Passenger tax to be levied on school buses
Abhay Jain

Gurgaon, February 23
The Haryana Government has withdrawn the passenger tax exemption on academic institutional buses from February 2006. In exercise of the powers conferred by Section 10 of the Punjab Passengers and Goods Taxation Act 1952 (Punjab Act 16 of 1952), the Governor of Haryana rescinded the order contained in the Excise and Taxation Department dated September 1970 regarding exemption to school buses.

As per the notification issued by Ramendra Jakhu, Haryana Financial Commissioner and Principal Secretary, Transport Department, the buses of educational institutions may in lieu of the tax chargeable on fare and freight pay a lump sum amount of Rs 60 per seat per month from July to March.

The District Transport Officer (DTO), Mr Narinder Singh Yadav, informed Â‘The TribuneÂ’ that the local office has already started sending notices to school authorities in this regard.

Â“We have identified 412 buses as per our record, which have been engaged by the school authorities. The tax for a 52-seat bus would be Rs 3,120 per month,Â” said Mr Yadav. The government has re-imposed the tax on buses of educational institutions as most of the institutions are running on profitable basis and not on charity basis as they used to run earlier, he added. Mr Yadav estimated that Gurgaon and Mewat would have as many as 500 buses engaged by schools.

In effect, more than Rs 15 lakh per month extra revenue would be collected on this account, he added.

However, a principal of a leading school said that Rs 60 per seat per month would result in an extra burden on the students, as the school authorities were not earning out of the transportation facilities provided to the students.

It would be better if the government charged a nominal amount as passenger tax from school authorities, he added.
 

Beware of property pre-launch offers 
Tribune News Service

Faridabad, February 23
Even as a number of pre-launch advertisements offering plots and flats for advance booking and purchase have been appearing in the media, people are finding it difficult to establish the genuineness of the offers. With the authorities yet to release the list of authorised builders, many of the prospective buyers fear that they could become victims of misinformation.

It may be recalled that advertisements regarding such offers have been appearing for the past several months in some of the leading dailies.

According to sources, the Department of Town and Country Planning has objected to some of the advertisements by certain companies, which had failed to obtain the necessary license.

According to a local resident who had applied against one of the offers, plots and flats have been oversold even though constructions are yet to begin.

When the Deputy Commissioner, Mrs G. Anupama, was contacted in this regard, she informed that she had come to know of certain irregularities in some of the offers and had prepared a detailed report for the higher authorities in Chandigarh. It is learnt that the Department of Town and Country Planning had issued notices to some of the builders and dealers making such offers in Rewari district.

According to sources, selling of apartments and plots under the pre-launch category had no legal standing and was a violation of the Punjab Schedule Roads and Controlled Area Restriction of Unregulated Development Act 1963 and the Haryana Urban Development Act 1975.

The Administrator, Haryana Urban Development Authority (HUDA), Mr Sudhir Rajpal, said that his office had nothing to do with the offers made by private builders or companies as the license in this regard was issued by the Department of Town and Country Planning.
 

Fog foxes Met office
Vibha Sharma
Tribune News Service

New Delhi, February 23
These days, a rather unusual and interesting weather phenomenon (it cannot be termed as freak, though) in Delhi has people as well as the Met office somewhat foxed when on certain days, morning fog descends upon the city.

It happened on Tuesday, on February 17 and the week before that when despite relatively high temperatures in the morning, the city remained enveloped in moderate fog till about 9 am.

Needless to add, this sudden development led to a delay in incoming and outgoing flights and also some confusion on roads. But what makes such events an unusual phenomenon is the fact that of the three essentials for the fog formation to take place on all the three days, two were not in its favour.

And of them one, temperature that is, particularly so. Fog, which is nothing but low clouds, occurs when three factors favour it: low temperatures, high humidity and no-wind condition. Some weathermen say that any one of these conditions has the power to veto fog.

However, despite Delhi recording a relatively high temperature in the morning, low to moderate fog descends upon the city, particularly the relatively open areas. Met officials agree that such occurrences do not happen quite often. They also agree it is unusual, though not a freak phenomenon.

Â“This has happened before,Â” they say, while admitting that fog usually descends when temperatures are low.

They are also quick to add that though the three factors are mandatory for fog formation, it is not as if their contribution has to be equal.

In somewhat complicated Met jargon, they explain how unequal combination of three factors can also lead to the formation of fog, They also add that there has been sufficient moisture in the air which is why even while the temperature was high, conditions become favourable for the optimisation process for the formation of fog.

Meanwhile, another explanation for occurrence of fog on relatively warm mornings could perhaps be the impact of pollution and urbanisation.

Some weathermen say that if condensation nuclei Â— dust particles or pollutants Â— are present in sufficient numbers in the atmosphere, condensation of the moisture in the air can comfortably take place around them.

With sufficiently high level of pollutants in the cityÂ’s air, maybe Delhiites could expect to see more of this phenomenon in the future.

Heads may roll in probe into co-op society scam
Parmindar Singh

Noida, February 23
The office-bearers of all the 145 residential housing societies, subject to an all-round probe, find themselves exposed on several fronts. Should the probe proceed on right lines, the heads of those who had indulged in frauds under the garb of housing societies may roll.

The CEO, Noida, Sanjeev Saran has written a letter to the Housing Commissioner of Avas Vikas Parishad in which the need for probing the activities of all the 145 group housing societies has been underlined by him.

Mr Saran has also called for the need to examine role of the members of the group housing societies. In case the Housing Commissioner orders such a probe, all the former and current members of the group societies will be subject to such an enquiry.

Such a probe will bring out the role of members who had misled the buyers of flats and committed financial frauds amounting to hundreds of crores.

The Noida Authority CEO has also suggested to the District Magistrate to order a probe at his level so that the revenue loss to the government running into hundreds of crores could be stopped.

Mr Sanjeev Saran has also called for a probe into the flats of defaulting group housing societies which have not been able to sell their flats so far.

The authority is likely to take back such flats into its possession. The CEO said some co-operative housing societies are also among defaulting group housing societies. Some allottees have lodged complaints with the Noida Authority against these societies.

Needless to add, this sudden development led to a delay in incoming and outgoing flights and also some confusion on roads. And of them one, temperature that is, particularly so. Fog, which is nothing but low clouds, occurs when three factors favour it: low temperatures, high humidity and no-wind condition. Some weathermen say that any one of these conditions has the power to veto fog.

However, despite Delhi recording a relatively high temperature in the morning, low to moderate fog descends upon the city, particularly the relatively open areas. Met officials agree that such occurrences do not happen quite often. They also agree it is unusual, though not a freak phenomenon.

Â“This has happened before,Â” they say, while admitting that fog usually descends when temperatures are low.

They are also quick to add that though the three factors are mandatory for fog formation, it is not as if their contribution has to be equal.

In somewhat complicated Met jargon, they explain how unequal combination of three factors can also lead to the formation of fog, They also add that there has been sufficient moisture in the air which is why even while the temperature was high, conditions become favourable for the optimisation process for the formation of fog.

Meanwhile, another explanation for occurrence of fog on relatively warm mornings could perhaps be the impact of pollution and urbanisation.

Some weathermen say that if condensation nuclei Â— dust particles or pollutants Â— are present in sufficient numbers in the atmosphere, condensation of the moisture in the air can comfortably take place around them.

With sufficiently high level of pollutants in the cityÂ’s air, maybe Delhiites could expect to see more of this phenomenon in the future.
