Bob Brown marks ten years as Greens rep

 PRINT FRIENDLY EMAIL STORY

AM - Thursday, 2 March , 2006  08:08:00

Reporter: Karen Barlow

KAREN PERCY: John Howard is not the only politician marking a 10 year milestone.

On this day in 1996 Tasmanians voted prominent conservationist Bob Brown into the Senate.

Back then he was the Greens' only representative. Now, he has three Greens colleagues in the Senate and his party out-polled the Democrats at the last election.

While there's been ongoing speculation, as we've just been hearing, about John Howard's possible retirement, Senator Brown says he's full of beans and ready for the next 10 years.

He's feeling so confident that he's predicting there'll be a Greens Government one day in Australia.

Senator Brown is speaking here with AM's Karen Barlow.

BOB BROWN: We're an opposition where very often the Labor Party provides none. It's a very important role we have in the Parliament.

No wonder. I came in here, there was one Australian Green. Then with Kerry Nettle there were two, then last election there were four.

And I'm really looking forward to next year's election, where we could go right to eight in the Senate and we're knocking on the door of the House of Representatives at every election.

KAREN BARLOW: Well, how is it for a party like the Greens with the balance of power going to the Government?

BOB BROWN: Made no difference. We've hardly been in the balance of power at any stage. But the Greens since I've been here have gone� doubled, tripled the rating with the electorate.

We've become the third major political party in the country and we're on our way to becoming a real power broker in this country and ultimately, the aim has to be becoming part of the Government of the country.

That's on� in the offing in Tasmania. We have got the credentials for that.

KAREN BARLOW: How seriously are the Greens taken in Canberra? Can the Greens seriously negotiate with the Government of the day?

BOB BROWN: Well, absolutely. The Prime Minister negotiated with me, for example, to ensure - through his intermediaries - that the Constitutional referendum on the Republic got up some years ago.

Similar with the need to stop online betting, in the way the Government legislated just a few years ago.

But more important than that is the pressure that we put on both the Government and Opposition to keep such issues as global warming on the front burner.

KAREN BARLOW: Senator Brown, how is your relationship with that other man celebrating 10 years as the head of a political party in Parliament?

BOB BROWN: I've sent John Howard a congratulations card, but I'm not holding my breath to get one back.

KAREN BARLOW: Other than sending a card, are you planning to catch up with John Howard?

BOB BROWN: Oh look, when I first got in here John Howard offered me a cup of tea and I went and saw him for an hour and I think he pretty quickly learnt he wasn't on the same wavelength with me. I've moved on from the 1950s, whereas he was still stuck there.

But I'll offer him a cup of tea and have offered him a cup of tea in the card I've sent and we'll see. I'll keep him a piece of chocolate cake.

KAREN PERCY: The Greens leader Bob Brown, speaking there with Karen Barlow.
