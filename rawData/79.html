Student protesters in Papua fear revenge attacks

 PRINT FRIENDLY EMAIL STORY

AM - Thursday, 23 March , 2006  08:24:00

Reporter: Peter Cave

TONY EASTLEY: Up to 1200 students are reported to be hiding in the hills around Jayapura, the capital of Indonesia's Papua Province, fearful of revenge attacks by members of the Indonesian Police Mobile Brigade known as Brimob.

It has a reputation for brutality in dealing with separatist conflicts, as our Foreign Affairs Editor Peter Cave reports from Jakarta.

PETER CAVE: According to Aloy Renwarin from the human rights groups Elsham in Jayapura, the 1200 students who live in dormitories at the state-run Cendrawasih University, which was at the centre of the clash last week, are in hiding.

He said they were hungry and some were in need of medical attention. The university remains closed, and the streets are tense. When asked to go on tape, however, he refused saying he feared reprisals.

Hans Magel is a spokesman for a local student association and he spoke by mobile phone from Timica, near the sit of the mine the students say is polluting the environment and tacitly condoning human rights abuses by the Indonesian Security forces, which it pays to protect it from locals displaced by the operations.

"The students are hiding in the jungle because they feel threatened," he says. "They are short of food, the conditions aren't sanitary. It's an emergency situation."

(Sound of gunshots, crowd panic)

Last week, Indonesian television footage showed police shooting directly at students in the university grounds, but the authorities still have not released details of casualties among the demonstrators.

Police have confirmed to reporters that members of Brimob involved in the clash, have been confined to barracks, and their weapons about 40 in all, have been taken from them for examination.

"I can't tell you exactly how many were shot," says Hans Magel. "In such traumatic circumstances, we ourselves are finding it difficult gathering casualty figures. The latest information we have is that 22 were seriously injured."

The Indonesian daily, Koran Tempo has quoted a spokesman for Indonesia's Defence Minister, Juwono Sudarsono, as saying the Minister believes that Australian Greens Senator Kerry Nettle was directly linked to last week's violence.

Senator Nettle says she's not applied to go to Papua, but would like to.

KERRY NETTLE: Civil society groups have invited me to go, but I haven't started the process of discussions with the Indonesians about whether or not that would be possible.

PETER CAVE: Do you have any links, direct or otherwise, to anyone who may have organised these protests?

KERRY NETTLE: No. I don't know what that comment is about. All of the West Papuan groups that I've met with have a commitment to resolving the conflict in West Papua non-violently.

TONY EASTLEY: Greens Senator Kerry Nettle. Peter Cave reporting from Jakarta there.
